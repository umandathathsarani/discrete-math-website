Question 1: The Goal of Counting

What is the primary purpose of mathematical counting in discrete math?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
To determine the number of elements in a set or the number of ways to arrange/choose objects without having to manually list every single possibility.

Question 2: Sum vs. Product Rule

If you see the word "AND" (or sequential tasks) in a word problem, which counting rule should you generally apply? What if you see the word "OR"?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
"AND" / Sequential = Product Rule (Multiplication).
"OR" / Mutually Exclusive = Sum Rule (Addition).

Question 3: Applying the Sum Rule

A student can choose a project from 5 Web Development topics OR 4 Artificial Intelligence topics. How many total choices does the student have?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Because the student chooses ONE topic in total (mutually exclusive), we use the Sum Rule: 5 + 4 = 9 possible choices.

Question 4: Applying the Product Rule

To access a magical vault, you must select 1 letter from the alphabet (26 options) followed by 1 digit (0-9, which is 10 options). How many different codes exist?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Because the tasks are sequential (Letter AND a Digit), we use the Product Rule: 26 × 10 = 260 possible codes.

Question 5: Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion

What is the mathematical formula for the Subtraction Rule (also known as the Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion)?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
|A ∪ B| = |A| + |B| - |A ∩ B|

Question 6: Applying the Subtraction Rule

In a class of 50 students, 30 study Java, 20 study Python, and 5 study BOTH. How many students study either Java OR Python?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
|Java ∪ Python| = 30 + 20 - 5 = 45 students.

Question 7: The Division Rule

If you arrange objects in a circle, rotating the circle doesn't create a new arrangement. If there are n ways to arrange items linearly, but each distinct circle is counted d times, how do you find the unique circular arrangements?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Using the Division Rule: n / d.

Question 8: Permutation Definition

What is the most critical defining feature of a Permutation compared to other counting methods?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Order matters! The arrangement (A, B, C) is counted as completely different from (C, B, A).

Question 9: Permutation Without Repetition Formula

State the formula for calculating permutations of r items chosen from n distinct items, without repetition.

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
P(n, r) = n! / (n - r)!

Question 10: Permutation Without Repetition Calculation

A club has 10 members. In how many ways can they elect a President, Vice President, and Secretary? (No person can hold more than one office).

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Since roles are specific, order matters. n = 10, r = 3.
P(10, 3) = 10! / (10 - 3)! = 10! / 7!
= 10 × 9 × 8 = 720 ways.

Question 11: Total Ordering

How many different ways can 5 distinct books be arranged on a shelf?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
This is a permutation of 5 items choosing all 5. P(5,5) = 5! / 0! = 5!
5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120 ways.

Question 12: Permutation With Repetition Formula

If you are forming an arrangement of length r from n available items, and you are allowed to repeat items, what is the formula?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
nr

Question 13: Permutation With Repetition Calculation

A digital lock requires a 4-digit PIN code using numbers 0-9. How many possible PINs can be created if numbers can be repeated?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Options (n) = 10, Slots (r) = 4.
104 = 10,000 possible PINs.

Question 14: Indistinguishable Objects Formula

What is the formula to find the number of distinct permutations of n objects where some objects (n1, n2, etc.) are identical to each other?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
n! / (n1! × n2! × ... × nk!)

Question 15: The "MISSISSIPPI" Problem

How many distinct ways can the letters of the word "SUCCESS" be arranged?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Total letters (n) = 7.
Identical letters: S appears 3 times, C appears 2 times.
Ways = 7! / (3! × 2!)
= 5040 / (6 × 2) = 5040 / 12 = 420 distinct ways.

Question 16: Tricky Indistinguishable Calculation

A wizard has 8 potion bottles in a row: 4 are exactly identical Red, 3 are identical Blue, and 1 is Green. How many distinct arrangements exist?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Total (n) = 8. Identical: 4 Red, 3 Blue.
8! / (4! × 3!) = (8 × 7 × 6 × 5) / (3 × 2 × 1)
= 1680 / 6 = 280 arrangements.

Question 17: Combining Rules I

A restaurant offers 3 appetizers, 5 main courses, and 2 desserts. If a meal consists of exactly one of each, how many different meals are possible?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
Using the Product Rule: 3 × 5 × 2 = 30 different meals.

Question 18: Evaluating "0!" in Counting

When calculating P(n, n) — meaning arranging all n items out of n items — the formula results in n! / 0!. What does this evaluate to and why?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
By mathematical definition, 0! = 1. Therefore, n! / 1 simply equals n!. This correctly matches the logic that arranging n items has n! possibilities.

Question 19: Complex Product Rule

How many bit strings (using only 0s and 1s) of length 8 are there?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
For each of the 8 positions, there are 2 choices (0 or 1). Using the Product Rule (or Permutation with Repetition):
28 = 256 bit strings.

Question 20: Permutation Logic Check

If you are picking a team of 3 students from a class of 20 to just go on a field trip together, should you use the Permutation formula? Why or why not?

🪄 Lumos! Reveal Answer

Answer:
No. Because all 3 students are just going on a trip together, the order they are chosen does not matter. You would use Combinations (Lecture 5!), not Permutations.
